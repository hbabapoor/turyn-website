[FAQ](#faq) --- ## What "Custom" Actually Means on the North Shore {#what-custom-means} Two entirely different types of business operate under the "custom home builder" banner on the North Shore. The branding is identical. The product is not. **Fully custom builders** start from your brief and your site. Every decision — floor plan, structural system, ceiling height, material specification — is designed for your specific block. These builders typically run 6–12 projects per year and will ask about your site before they show you anything. Anything. If you hear "what's the block like?" in the first three minutes of a conversation, that's a good sign. **Semi-custom builders** work from a library of base designs they modify. Facades get updated. Non-load-bearing walls move. Specifications upgrade. These are legitimate businesses that deliver genuine value on the right site — but the result is a modified project home, not a ground-up custom design. The distinction matters on the North Shore, where steep blocks, heritage overlays, and DA requirements that sometimes involve more consultants than a mid-size litigation tend to make base-plan libraries unworkable. Here is the most reliable tell in the industry: if a builder shows you a catalogue before asking about your site, you have now learned everything you need to know about them. Take the brochure — it's usually quite nice — and don't return their calls with quite the same urgency. --- ## How to Vet a Builder Before You Meet Them {#vet-a-builder} ![Architect with hard hat reviewing plans at a modern home exterior](https://images.pexels.com/photos/7937745/pexels-photo-7937745.jpeg?auto=compress&cs=tinysrgb&dpr=2&h=650&w=940) *Photo by [Pavel Danilyuk](https://www.pexels.com/@pavel-danilyuk) via Pexels* Do this homework before the first phone call, not after you've already had three good meetings and are emotionally attached to the renders. **Check the NSW Fair Trading licence.** Any builder working on residential projects over $20,000 in NSW must hold a current contractor licence. Search the [NSW Fair Trading licence register](https://www.fairtrading.nsw.gov.au/trades-and-businesses/contractor-licences) using the builder's business or trading name. Confirm it is current, not suspended, and — this part matters — matches the legal entity that will actually sign your contract. These three things are not always the same. **Read the NCAT record.** The NSW Civil and Administrative Tribunal publishes its decisions. A builder with multiple NCAT appearances for defects or non-completion is showing you a pattern, not a run of bad luck. One resolved dispute from years ago is different. Five are not. **Visit a completed project — not a display home.** Ask for contact details of clients who moved in three or four years ago. Call those people directly. Ask what went wrong (something always goes wrong), how the builder responded, and whether they'd build with them again. A builder who won't provide references is, in a way, also providing a reference. **Find out who actually manages your site.** Here is a detail some clients discover only after signing: the principal whose portfolio you fell in love with will often assign your build to a site manager you have never met. That manager's experience, communication habits, and tolerance for your 6pm calls about tile grout is the product you are actually buying. Meet them before you sign. **Ask about subcontractor continuity.** A builder who has used the same carpenter, tiler, and electrician across jobs for a decade delivers more consistent quality than one who re-prices trades on each project. It's not glamorous due diligence, but it's the kind that saves arguments in month 14. --- ## What North Shore Custom Builds Cost in 2026 {#costs} ![Aerial view of a luxury custom home with manicured gardens](https://images.pexels.com/photos/9150612/pexels-photo-9150612.jpeg?auto=compress&cs=tinysrgb&dpr=2&h=650&w=940) *Photo by [Erik Mclean](https://www.pexels.com/@erikmclean) via Pexels* North Shore custom builds in 2026 sit across three broad tiers. These figures are for construction only — we'll come to the other surprises in a moment. | Tier | Construction cost per m² | What you get | |------|--------------------------|--------------| | Entry custom | $3,500–$4,200 | Custom layout, standard-grade fixtures, builder's preferred suppliers | | Mid custom | $4,200–$5,500 | Architectural finishes, engineered timber, full builder–architect coordination | | High-end custom | $5,500–$8,000+ | Full architectural intent realised, premium imported materials, bespoke joinery, complex structural features | Now, about those other surprises. The construction rate above excludes: - Land (which, on the North Shore, requires no further comment) - Demolition and asbestos removal — distressingly common on older North Shore blocks - Development Application and Construction Certificate fees - Architect, structural engineer, geotechnical engineer, and surveyor (yes, all of them) - Landscaping, pool, driveway, fencing - Furnishings and the styling consultation you'll be talked into On a typical 600–750m² North Shore block, those additional costs regularly add $250,000–$500,000 to the total. A well-specified custom home on the North Shore reaches $3M–$4M all-in before the land is on the ledger. This is not a complaint. It is arithmetic. The most common budget error — and it is committed constantly — is taking a cost-per-m² figure from a flat block and applying it to a steep, split-level site. Challenging topography costs materially more to build on. The structural complexity is different. The scaffolding costs are different. The concrete pump hire is different. If a builder quotes you the flat-block rate on a 1:4 slope without flagging this, ask them to explain the provisional sums for site works in writing. --- ## The Realistic Timeline {#timeline} ![New home under construction — framing stage](https://images.pexels.com/photos/37627682/pexels-photo-37627682.jpeg?auto=compress&cs=tinysrgb&dpr=2&h=650&w=940) *Photo by [D Goug](https://www.pexels.com/@d-goug) via Pexels* A full custom home build on the North Shore, from initial brief to handover, typically runs 24–36 months. If someone has told you 18 months, ask them to show their working. | Phase | Typical duration | |-------|-----------------| | Concept design and brief development | 2–4 months | | Development Application lodgement and determination | 6–18 months | | Construction Certificate | 1–2 months | | Construction | 12–18 months | | Defects period and handover | 1–3 months | The DA phase is where timelines go to become suggestions. Heritage conservation areas cover large parts of Mosman, Hunters Hill, and Lane Cove LGAs. Applications affecting heritage items or sitting within conservation areas routinely require additional reports — heritage impact statements, arborist reports, acoustic reports, neighbour notifications — each of which adds time. Mosman Council officially targets 40 days for straightforward DAs. Heritage-affected properties regularly run 6–12 months. Treat 40 days the way you treat the "15 minutes or less" estimate at the DMV: theoretically possible, not the base case. If your project requires a rezoning, planning proposal, or SEPP variation, add another 6–12 months to that. Budget for the longer end of every range. The builders promising 18-month all-in timelines on heritage-affected North Shore sites are either not accounting for DA risk or are not familiar with the councils. Neither is a good sign. --- ## When a Custom Builder Is the Wrong Choice {#wrong-choice} This section is the one most guides skip. We are not most guides. A fully custom builder is genuinely not the right fit for every project — and being honest about that early saves everyone considerable time and goodwill. Here is when to look elsewhere: - **Your budget is under $900,000 all-in for new construction.** A custom builder will take your call, give you a warm meeting, and never quite get back to you with the same enthusiasm. At that budget, a semi-custom or volume builder delivers better value. Custom builders carry overhead structures that price their minimum viable project above this level. That is not a criticism — it is just how the maths works. - **Your site is flat, rectangular, and carries no heritage or planning complications.** A project home on an easy block can deliver 75–80% of a custom outcome for 55–60% of the cost. If your brief is not complex and your site is not complex, you may be paying the complexity premium for nothing. - **You need to be in the house within 14 months.** The DA process alone on most North Shore LGAs makes this unrealistic. If you have a hard deadline — lease expiry, school enrolment, a deal you made with yourself — a custom build is likely the wrong vehicle. It is not the builder's fault. It is the council's timeline. - **You cannot commit regular time across an 18-month design and specification process.** Custom builds require 30–50 hours of active client involvement — design workshops, selections, specification sign-offs, consultant reviews. If your schedule genuinely won't support that, decisions stall, builders wait, and the cost of that waiting shows up on your next progress claim. None of these answers are anything to be embarrassed about. The right builder for your project is the one matched to your actual constraints — not the most prestigious name on the North Shore. --- ## Questions to Ask Before Signing {#questions} A builder who handles these questions well is showing you something. A builder who deflects, shortens, or somehow turns every question back into a conversation about their award nominations is also showing you something. 1. How many projects are currently under construction, and how many are confirmed in your pipeline for the next 12 months? (More than 8 active projects from a small firm is a capacity risk for your site supervision — the maths are unavoidable.) 2. Who will be my primary contact during the build — you personally, a project manager, or an administrator? Can I meet that person before we sign? 3. Can you give me contact details for the last three clients you handed over to, including one from at least two years ago? 4. What is your process for the 13-week post-handover defects inspection, and what is the typical response time for rectification? 5. What contract type do you use — HIA, MBA, or your own? Under what circumstances do you recommend cost-plus versus fixed-price? 6. How are subcontractor payments managed during the build, and how are subbies protected if a dispute arises mid-project? Six questions. Not unreasonable for a $2M+ commitment. If the meeting runs long because of them, that is a feature, not a problem. --- ## FAQ {#faq} **How do I find reputable custom home builders on the North Shore?** Start with the [Housing Industry Association](https://hia.com.au) and [Master Builders Association NSW](https://www.mbansw.asn.au) member directories — both require ongoing professional development for membership, which filters out a share of the field. Then verify the contractor licence on the NSW Fair Trading register and check NCAT decisions for any defect or non-payment disputes. The combination of all three gives you a reasonable picture before the first conversation. **Do I need my own architect, or does the builder provide one?** Some custom builders have in-house architects; others coordinate with independent practices. If you have a complex brief, a heritage site, or a particular design vision you'd like someone in your corner to defend, engaging your own architect — who works for you, not the builder — gives you a much stronger position on both quality and design intent throughout the build. **What is a cost-plus contract and should I use one?** A cost-plus contract means you pay the builder's actual costs plus an agreed margin (typically 15–20%). More transparency, less cost certainty. On highly complex North Shore projects, it can be the right structure. On standard custom builds, a fixed-price contract with clearly defined provisional sum allowances is the safer starting point — it puts the cost risk where it belongs, rather than where it drifts. **How long does a DA take in North Shore LGAs?** Mosman targets 40 days for straightforward applications. Heritage-affected properties regularly run 6–12 months. Ku-ring-gai and Lane Cove follow similar patterns. Budget a minimum of 8 months from lodgement to determination. Treat anything faster as a genuine bonus rather than the base expectation. **What is a realistic all-in budget for a custom home on the North Shore?** A mid-tier custom build in 2026 runs approximately $4,200–$5,500/m² for construction. A 400m² home at the midpoint costs roughly $1.94M to build before a single consultant, DA fee, or landscape quote is added. With everything included — demolition, consultants, fees, landscaping, pool, furnishings — a well-specified custom home on the North Shore regularly sits between $3M and $4.5M, excluding the cost of the land. That figure surprises people who did the cost-per-m² calculation on a napkin.
