# Humour Style Guide

Derived from: https://www.hireawriter.us/creative/writing-humor-the-art-of-making-readers-laugh
First applied in: `blog-custom-home-builders-north-shore.md`

---

## The Core Rule: Sprinkle, Don't Saturate

Humor functions like hot sauce — it works because it's not everywhere. Apply it at:
- The opening paragraph (hooks attention, sets tone)
- Section transitions (signals a shift from dry to earnest)
- One well-placed aside per major section
- The punchline at the end of a long technical explanation

Never force it. If a joke doesn't arrive naturally, leave the paragraph straight.

---

## Technique 1: The Self-Aware Parody Opener

Start with a pop culture parody or cultural reference that's slightly embarrassing to make — then lean into the embarrassment. The self-awareness is the joke.

**Template:** [Famous line reworked for the topic] + brief acknowledgement that you just did that.

**Example used:**
> "I've got 99 problems, but finding a genuine custom home builder on the North Shore shouldn't be one. And yet, here we are."

**How to apply:** Works best in the first two sentences. The reference does not need to be clever — it needs to be recognisable. The reader's groan IS the connection.

---

## Technique 2: The Dry Cultural Comparison

Compare an industry-specific term or practice to something the reader already understands from everyday life. Keep it deadpan — no winking, no "get it?".

**Template:** "[Industry term] is like [everyday thing] — [the punchline that exposes the absurdity]."

**Examples used:**
> "'Custom' in the building industry has become a bit like 'artisanal' at the supermarket — it means roughly whatever the person selling it wants it to mean."

> "Treat 40 days the way you treat the '15 minutes or less' estimate at the DMV: theoretically possible, not the base case."

**How to apply:** One per major section maximum. Works especially well when explaining a term the reader already suspects is slippery (e.g. "custom", "boutique", "luxury").

---

## Technique 3: The Parenthetical Aside

Insert a dry, brief observation inside parentheses or em dashes that undercuts or adds texture to the straight sentence around it. Keep it short — the aside should be shorter than the sentence it's interrupting.

**Template:** Straight statement — (wry observation) — continue straight.

**Examples used:**
> "Take the brochure — it's usually quite nice — and don't return their calls with quite the same urgency."

> "Land (which, on the North Shore, requires no further comment)"

> "Furnishings and the styling consultation you'll be talked into"

> "A builder who won't provide references is, in a way, also providing a reference."

**How to apply:** Use once or twice per post — usually in lists or when you want to acknowledge something the reader is already thinking.

---

## Technique 4: The [Switches to Straight Face] Transition

After the humorous opener, explicitly signal the shift to earnest content. This validates the reader's attention and sets up the rest of the post as trustworthy.

**Exact phrasing used:**
> "[Switches to straight face.] Everything from here is practical — what these builds actually cost, how long the DA really takes, and when a custom builder is the wrong tool for the job entirely."

**Variations for future posts:**
- "[Right. Straight face now.]"
- "[Serious mode engaged.]"
- "That's the joke out of the way. Here is the part you actually searched for."

**How to apply:** Once per post, immediately after the opening. Never use it mid-post — the transition back to serious should happen only once.

---

## Technique 5: The Understated Punchline

Build to a factual conclusion with a deadpan kicker. Works best after a list of technical items where the reader expects a dry summary.

**Template:** [Straight factual statement]. [Short deadpan kicker that names what just happened.]

**Examples used:**
> "A well-specified custom home on the North Shore reaches $3M–$4M all-in before the land is on the ledger. This is not a complaint. It is arithmetic."

> "Six questions. Not unreasonable for a $2M+ commitment. If the meeting runs long because of them, that is a feature, not a problem."

> "That figure surprises people who did the cost-per-m² calculation on a napkin. It should not surprise you."

**How to apply:** Works well at the end of a cost or timeline section where you've just delivered sobering numbers. The kicker acknowledges the reader's reaction without wallowing in it.

---

## Technique 6: The Self-Aware Section Flag

Call out what you're doing as you do it — particularly when the section is one most guides avoid. This builds trust and makes the reader feel they're getting the unfiltered version.

**Examples used:**
> "This section is the one most guides skip. We are not most guides."

> "Do this homework before the first phone call, not after you've already had three good meetings and are emotionally attached to the renders."

**How to apply:** Once per post, usually at the start of the most contrarian or uncomfortable section (e.g. "when not to hire us", "the realistic timeline", "what this actually costs").

---

## What to Avoid

- Exclamation marks — they deflate the deadpan effect entirely
- Jokes that require the reader to share obscure cultural knowledge
- More than one humor technique per paragraph
- Humor in FAQ answers — keep those straight and direct
- Humor in cost tables or timeline tables — the format already earns trust, don't undercut it
- Apologising for the joke (saying "jokes aside..." defeats the sprinkle approach)
- Emojis — they belong in a different register entirely

---

## Rhythm Pattern

The sentence rhythm that carries the humor:

1. Long setup sentence that earns the reader's attention
2. Short punchy follow-on (the joke or kicker)
3. Return to normal sentence length

**Example:**
> "The DA phase is where timelines go to become suggestions." ← joke
> "Heritage conservation areas cover large parts of Mosman, Hunters Hill, and Lane Cove LGAs." ← back to straight

Think of it as: wind up, release, continue. The release (short sentence) is where the humor lands.

---

## Tone Anchor

The persona is: a knowledgeable friend who has seen the industry from the inside, finds its quirks mildly absurd, and would rather tell you the uncomfortable truth than keep things polished and vague. Not cynical. Not sarcastic. Dry and warm at the same time.

The reader should feel like they've been let in on something — not lectured to, not sold to.
